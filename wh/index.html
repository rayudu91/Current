Basic Information

1. What is the Heat Pump Water Heater?

A heat pump water heater uses electricity to move heat from surrounding air to the tank water instead of heating the water directly. This process makes them three to four times more energy efficient than conventional electric resistance water heaters and can cut water heating costs by up to 70% compared to a conventional electric water heater. (The savings are less when compared to a conventional natural gas water heater.) From a user's perspective, a HPWH operates similarly to a conventional water heater.

2. What is a hybrid HPWH?

A typical HPWH is sometimes referred to as a hybrid HPWH. This means that it has an electric resistance heating element as a backup to the heat pump technology. If water needs to be heated faster than the heat pump is capable of (such as when there's an unusually great need for hot water), the resistance element can be used to reduce the time required to make hot water available.

3. What tank sizes are available for HPWHs?

HPWHs come with different tank sizes. The most common sizes currently on the market are 50, 65, and 80 gallon units. Smaller systems, such as 40 gallon units, are also becoming available. Under normal circumstances, consumers replacing a conventional water heater with a heat pump water heater of the same size should not notice a significant change in the amount of hot water available.

4. What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a HPWH?
Advantages:
  • Use a proven, energy-efficient technology to heat water.
  • When replacing a natural gas water heater, there will be a substantial reduction in your carbon footprint. In addition, you will likely enjoy a reduction in energy costs, but it may not be substantial. Pairing the HPWH with a solar array can generate substantial energy cost savings compared to a conventional gas water heater.
  • If you currently have an electric water heater, switching to a HPWH can reduce your electric water heating costs by up to 70% and may also reduce your homeâ€™s carbon footprint.
Disadvantages:
  • Higher up-front cost when compared to conventional tank-type water heaters. Rebates are sometimes available, however, to reduce the up-front cost disadvantage.
  • If replacing a natural gas water heater, wiring will need to be run to the unit. Most HPWHs require 240 V electricity, but some are being developed that will function with 120 V.
  • Noisier than conventional water heaters, sounding similar to an air conditioner. Accordingly, they may not be as well suited if located in an interior space (such as an interior closet).
5. Is a tankless electric water heater more efficient?

No. Tankless electric water heaters can be at most 100% efficient as they directly heat the water with electricity. A HPWH just moves heat and can be effectively 300-400% efficient. The efficiency rating can be obtained by multiplying the EF (Energy Factor) rating of the HPWH by 100.
